The new path of direct photo­graphy

While some photographers were pursuing imaginary worlds, others contented themselves with direct, untouched shots with a classical reportage style: the setting of life, people behaviour. However Robert Frank had already created these images and the time for images to symbolize great timeless truths was over, for we can´t record without passing judgement.

Two notable photographers of 60´s and 70´s were Lee Friedlander and Garry Winogrand, both using the rules of spontaneity for their artistic construction, similar to a jazz musician in their virtuosity of deconstructing the rules. Garry Winogrand, originally a painter, abandoned his art because it was too slow and became a reportage photographer for the Prix agency. He set out across America in a similar travel to Robert Frank, however Winogrand loved human contact and frequently used tools such as zoos, pretty girls and socialite gatherings simply as pretexts to produce images which he himself regarded as “still-life´s”. His books, The Animals (1969), Women are Beautiful (1975) and Public Relations (1977) are brilliant examples of this, devoid of any commentary one might be tempted to supply.

Although in art at the time, the abstract aesthetic began to evolve. Artists such as Tobey, Pollock, Bissière and Vieira da Silva began evoking the inextricable abundance of certain scenes. Meanwhile in photography, the intense disorder of the urban landscape defied the classic reporter seeking the right angle and moment for a clearly stated image. Friedlander was to construct his photographs by emphasising the very confusion of the spectacle, but unlike Winogrand, always with a solitary outlook. An exhibition organized in 1966 by Nathan Lyons Toward a Social Landscape was to show the works of Winogrand, Friedlander and Duane Michals. In these photographic spaces, man seemed to have been rejected and replaced by cars, posters, monuments and posts. Recognition that meaning has been lost in a world in which eventhough mankind multiples and gathers he still remains invisible; the human being has become just one object among many others.

Until now photography had tried to eliminate whatever stood in the way of its theme but Friedlander photographs precisely all that is in the way, beginning with his own shadow, since it happens to be there—in Self-Portraits, 1970. In American Monumnet (1975), Friedlander assembles a collection of images of official monuments, devoid of lyricism, intention or meaning. It´s concern is what is photographed, not any perceived idea of what a photograph should look like. Just as Winogrand states “I photograph to find out what something will look like photographed.” So too does Friedlander adopt this style which marked a whole line of direct photography and took over from the classic reportage of the thirties, forties and fifties. The title of Elliot Erwitt´s book, Photographs and Anti-photograph (1972) indicates the extent to which the new manner of photography sometimes seemed paradoxical even to those who practiced it. In Erwitt´s work the anxiety prompted by Robert Frank´s heritage is laced with a sense of humor. The American photographer seizes, with an equal exactitude and impassivity, upon the tense relations between things and people in modern life.

Working with a more brutal register, Mark Cohen seizes upon faces and bodies as they loom up in sudden light of a flash. The world of chaotic American suburbs tackled by Friedlander is also depicted, in the realm of color, by Stephen Shore. Because of fixity of the photographic image and the precise and haunting nature of it´s testimony, the objective truth it contains is always on the point of slipping into a hallucinatory dream. Joel Meyerowitz (Cape Light, 1978) another photographer of superb color and proximity, prefers busy crowds frozen in the electric light of a “retarded flash” which brings out the drawn expressions of the faces of several New York workers during rush hour. The work of Ralph Gibson, born 1939, assistant to the great Dorothea Lange and a friend and collaborator of Robert Frank, provides the most striking illustration of new direct photography. In 1969 Gibson set up his own publishing house, Lustrum Press. Each of his books of photography becomes an autonomous means of expression, making its effect through the succession, juxtaposition and rhythm of images. His Famous trilogy, The Somnambulist (1970), Déjà Vu (1973) and Days at Sea (1974), may be seem as a model for the art of putting images together.

Between The Somnambulist and Days at Sea a controlled shift takes place, moving away from visions still surrealistic and dreamlike towards almost minimal foreground form, veritable photographic bas-reliefs. But the tension between the oneiric content and the material presence is never broken. His endeavour is central to the double nature of photographic expression: on the one hand it can convey the imaginary; on the other it can report it.

In Tokyo, Ikko Narahara had his first work exhibited and was considered “the first phase of realism”. Soon Ikko joined Shomei Tomatsu, Eikoh Hosoe and others to an agency known as Vivo. This agency saw the rise of new direct photography outlooks in Asia until 1962 when Ikko set off on a long world trip and subsequently frequented the United States many times. He was as sensitive to the presence of the past i.e. World War II, (Where Time has Stopped, 1967), as he was to the timeless modernity of America (Where Time has vanished, 1975). But central to his work was space; whether enclosed and confined or wide open to the great expanses. In a cruder sense, Shomei Tomatsu combined his commitment to the aftermath of war (11.02 Hagasaki, 1962) and the social crisis of Japan (Oh, Shinjuku! 1962) with an intensely personal plastic means of expression. In his case too, human testimony and plastic abstraction ceased to clash.
